Vocabulary in context is a reading task before it is a memory task. An unfamiliar word is surrounded by grammar, contrast, examples, and tone. Those clues often reduce four possible meanings to one useful approximation.

## First identify the word’s job

Cover the target word and read the sentence. What grammatical form belongs in the gap?

- A word after an article such as *a* or *the* is often a noun.
- A word that modifies a noun is often an adjective.
- A word after a modal such as *can* or *may* is usually a base-form verb.
- A word that describes how an action happens may be an adverb.

This step will not reveal the definition, but it prevents you from comparing answers that cannot fit the sentence.

## Search four clue types

Use the nearest strong clue first:

1. **Definition or restatement:** commas, dashes, or phrases such as *that is* may explain the word.
2. **Contrast:** *but*, *however*, *although*, and *unlike* signal an opposing meaning.
3. **Example:** *for example* and *such as* show what belongs to a category.
4. **Cause and tone:** a result may show whether the word means increase, decrease, benefit, harm, approval, or doubt.

Do not force every clue to agree with a precise dictionary definition. Your first goal is a meaning accurate enough for the sentence.

## Worked example: contrast

**Practice text:** Early models treated the wetland as a stable environment. Later observations, however, showed that water levels were highly **variable**, rising after storms and falling during dry periods.

The contrast marker *however* tells us the later description challenges *stable*. The examples *rising* and *falling* confirm change over time. A useful replacement is **changeable**, not merely *wet* or *unpredictable in every way*.

## Worked example: restatement

**Practice text:** The shells were remarkably **durable**—several remained intact after decades of exposure to wind and salt.

The dash introduces evidence: the shells remained intact for decades. The target word must mean able to last or resist damage. You do not need the word’s history or every possible synonym.

## Verify by substitution

Replace the target word with your predicted meaning and reread the full sentence. Check three things:

1. Grammar: does the replacement fit the word form?
2. Logic: does it preserve the relationship between clauses?
3. Tone: is it too positive, negative, or extreme?

If “changeable” works but “chaotic” makes the sentence much stronger, keep the narrower meaning.

## Avoid three common traps

Word roots are hints, not verdicts. A familiar root can point in the wrong direction when a word has developed a specialized meaning.

Topic knowledge is also dangerous. The correct meaning must fit this sentence, even if you know the term can mean something else in another subject.

Finally, do not stop at a broad positive/negative guess when the options require a relationship such as *cause*, *reduce*, *temporary*, or *uncertain*. Direction is the start of the inference, not always the finish.

## A repeatable practice routine

For ten minutes, select five unfamiliar words from one short text. For each word, record:

- grammatical role;
- strongest clue type;
- approximate meaning;
- substituted sentence;
- dictionary check after the attempt.

The dictionary comes last so you can test the reading process. Mark whether your approximation was sufficient, even if it was not the perfect definition. Over time, count which clue type you overlook most often and build the next drill around it.

## Quick decision card

When time is limited, use this order: word job, nearest connector, local example, substitution, answer choice. That sequence keeps the reasoning inside the passage and turns vocabulary into a visible, reviewable process.
