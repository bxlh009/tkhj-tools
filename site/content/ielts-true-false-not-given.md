The official IELTS distinction is exact: **False** means the statement contradicts the text, while **Not Given** means the text neither confirms nor contradicts it. The difficult part is resisting information that feels likely but never appears.

## Convert the statement into testable parts

Underline the subject, the main claim, and any limiter such as *all*, *only*, *before*, *more*, or *because*. A statement can match the topic while changing one decisive detail.

For example, “The museum offers free entry to all students on Fridays” contains four testable parts: museum, free entry, all students, Fridays. A passage that mentions discounted Friday entry does not confirm the statement.

## Use the three-way decision test

After locating the relevant lines, ask in order:

1. Does the text clearly express the same meaning? Choose **True**.
2. Does the text clearly express the opposite meaning? Choose **False**.
3. Does the text leave the key point unanswered? Choose **Not Given**.

Do not choose False merely because you cannot find confirmation. False requires a contradiction.

## Worked example: missing cause

**Practice text:** The town opened a second cycle route in May. Bicycle use increased during the summer, while bus use remained stable.

**Statement:** Bicycle use increased because the new route was safer.

The text confirms an increase and mentions a new route, but it does not state why cycling increased or evaluate safety. The cause is **Not Given**. Calling it False would require text showing a different cause or that the route was not safer.

## Worked example: changed quantity

**Practice text:** Most workshop participants completed the online module before attending the practical session. A small group completed it the following week.

**Statement:** Every participant completed the module before the practical session.

The passage explicitly says a small group completed it later. “Every” is contradicted, so the answer is **False**.

## Paraphrase without widening meaning

IELTS rarely repeats the same wording. Train relationships:

- *a minority* can match *a small proportion*;
- *declined slightly* can match *showed a modest fall*;
- *was permitted* can match *was allowed*;
- *may have contributed* does not match *was the main cause*.

Write the relationship in simple words before selecting an answer. This prevents one shared keyword from hiding a changed degree or cause.

## Control outside knowledge

A statement may be factually true in the real world and still be Not Given. Imagine a text about polar research that never mentions temperature. “The researchers worked in freezing conditions” sounds obvious, but the task tests the text, not general knowledge.

Use a strict evidence note: copy or paraphrase the exact line that controls the answer. If you cannot write that note, you have not finished the question.

## Review mistakes by type

Label each error with one of these causes:

1. missed a limiter;
2. confused no evidence with contradiction;
3. accepted a changed cause;
4. imported outside knowledge;
5. matched words instead of meaning.

Then rewrite the statement so each of the three answers would be correct. This exercise reveals the boundary:

- True: “Bicycle use increased during the summer.”
- False: “Bicycle use decreased during the summer.”
- Not Given: “Tourists caused the increase in bicycle use.”

## Final evidence check

Before moving on, point to the controlling phrase and say one sentence: same meaning, opposite meaning, or unanswered. The label should follow the evidence; it should not replace it.
