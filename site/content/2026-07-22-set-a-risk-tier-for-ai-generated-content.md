Artificial intelligence tools can generate text, code, and images at scale, but not all outputs carry the same potential for harm. Treating every piece of AI-generated content as equally risky leads to either excessive friction in low-stakes scenarios or dangerous oversights in high-stakes ones. To manage this efficiently, editorial teams and product managers need a structured way to classify content before it reaches an audience.

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) provides a foundational framework for this classification through its AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF). By applying the logic within this framework, organizations can move from vague anxiety about AI to concrete, tiered review processes. This article outlines how to set risk tiers using four specific criteria: consequence, audience, reversibility, and source availability.

## The NIST Foundation for Risk Classification

The primary evidence for structuring risk management comes from the **NIST AI Risk Management Framework**. NIST defines risk in the context of AI as the "potential for adverse effects on individuals, groups, organizations, or society that arise from the development, deployment, or use of AI systems" [1].

Crucially, NIST does not prescribe a single method for assessing risk. Instead, it offers a voluntary framework designed to help organizations map their specific contexts. For content generation, this means the "risk" is not inherent to the technology itself, but rather to how the output is used and who interacts with it.

According to NIST’s guidance, effective risk management requires understanding the lifecycle of the AI system and the specific functions it performs [1]. When applied to editorial workflows, the "function" is content creation, and the "lifecycle" stage is publication. The framework encourages organizations to categorize risks based on their potential impact, which directly informs the depth of human review required.

## Four Criteria for Setting Risk Tiers

To operationalize NIST’s principles, we can break down risk assessment into four actionable dimensions. These dimensions allow you to assign a tier (Low, Medium, High) to any piece of AI-generated content.

### 1. Consequence
What happens if the content is incorrect, biased, or misleading?
*   **Low Consequence:** The content is informational or entertainment-focused with no direct impact on health, safety, finances, or legal standing. Example: A blog post about local coffee shops.
*   **High Consequence:** Errors could lead to physical harm, financial loss, legal liability, or significant reputational damage. Example: Medical advice, legal disclaimers, or financial investment recommendations.

### 2. Audience
Who is consuming the content, and what is their ability to verify it?
*   **General/Public Audience:** The readership is broad and diverse, including those with varying levels of digital literacy. High scrutiny is needed because misinformation spreads faster and affects more people.
*   **Expert/Niche Audience:** The readers are professionals who can critically evaluate claims or have domain-specific knowledge. While verification is still important, the baseline expectation of accuracy may be higher among the consumers themselves.

### 3. Reversibility
Can the error be corrected easily after publication?
*   **Irreversible or Hard-to-Correct:** The content is distributed via channels where edits are difficult or impossible (e.g., printed materials, certain social media algorithms, or archived news feeds). High risk requires pre-publication review.
*   **Reversible:** The content lives on a platform where updates, corrections, or retracts are instantaneous and visible (e.g., a live-updating dashboard or a standard CMS with edit history). Lower risk may allow for post-publication monitoring.

### 4. Source Availability
How easy is it to verify the facts generated by the AI?
*   **Verifiable Sources:** The topic relies on established, publicly available data (e.g., weather reports, sports scores, historical dates). If the AI hallucinates, it is easy to cross-reference with official sources.
*   **Unverifiable or Proprietary Sources:** The content involves subjective analysis, internal company data, or emerging trends with no clear authoritative reference. High risk requires human judgment to ensure coherence and factual grounding.

## Concrete Decision Workflow

Based on these criteria, you can implement a simple three-tier workflow. This approach aligns with NIST’s recommendation to tailor risk management activities to the specific context of use [1].

### Tier 1: Low Risk (Automated or Light Touch)
*   **Criteria:** Low consequence, expert audience, highly reversible, verifiable sources.
*   **Action:** Publish with minimal review. A quick scan for obvious errors or tone issues is sufficient.
*   **Example:** An internal memo summarizing meeting notes generated by AI.

### Tier 2: Medium Risk (Standard Editorial Review)
*   **Criteria:** Moderate consequence, general audience, somewhat reversible, partially verifiable sources.
*   **Action:** Require human editing for factual accuracy, tone, and clarity. Check key claims against at least one reliable source.
*   **Example:** A marketing blog post about industry trends.

### Tier 3: High Risk (Rigorous Human Oversight)
*   **Criteria:** High consequence, vulnerable/general audience, irreversible or hard-to-reverse, unverified or complex sources.
*   **Action:** Treat AI output as a draft only. A subject matter expert must verify all facts, check for bias, and ensure compliance with legal or ethical standards.
*   **Example:** A patient information sheet regarding medication side effects.

## When to Use It

This tiering system is most effective when:
*   You are scaling content production using AI tools.
*   Your organization handles sensitive topics (health, finance, law).
*   You need to allocate limited human reviewer resources efficiently.
*   You are establishing a consistent brand voice and quality standard across multiple contributors.

By explicitly defining risk tiers, you create a repeatable process that reduces cognitive load for editors. Instead of asking "Is this safe?", they ask "Which tier does this fall under?"

## When to Skip It

Do not apply rigid tiering when:
*   The content is purely creative or artistic with no factual claims.
*   The context is highly experimental or ephemeral (e.g., a one-time internal brainstorming session).
*   Legal or regulatory requirements mandate uniform high-level review regardless of perceived risk (in such cases, always default to the highest standard).

## Limits and Uncertainty

It is important to recognize the boundaries of this framework. The NIST AI Risk Management Framework is a voluntary guideline, not a legal regulation [1]. Therefore, compliance with this tiering system does not guarantee immunity from legal liability or public backlash.

Furthermore, risk is dynamic. A topic considered "low consequence" today may become "high consequence" tomorrow due to changes in public sentiment, new regulations, or emerging scientific understanding. For example, AI-generated content about a novel virus might start as informational but quickly become high-risk if misinformation spreads.

Additionally, the "source availability" criterion assumes that reliable sources exist. In rapidly evolving fields or niche industries, authoritative sources may be scarce or contradictory, making verification difficult even for human experts. In these cases, the risk tier should be elevated to account for the uncertainty.

Finally, this model focuses on content *output*. It does not address the risks associated with the *input* (prompt engineering) or the *model* itself (bias in training data). A comprehensive risk strategy must also consider these upstream factors, as recommended by NIST’s broader governance functions [1].
