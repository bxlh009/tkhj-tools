IELTS Writing Task 2 asks for a connected response of at least 250 words, and the official band descriptors organize assessment around task response, coherence and cohesion, lexical resource, and grammatical range and accuracy. Your final review should use the same categories instead of searching randomly for “better words.”

## Minute one: verify the task response

Return to the prompt and underline every required action. Did you discuss both views, answer both questions, explain advantages and disadvantages, or state a clear opinion?

Check that your position is visible in the introduction and consistent in the conclusion. A sophisticated paragraph cannot repair an essay that answers only half the prompt.

Use this short list:

1. Every part of the prompt is answered.
2. My position is unambiguous.
3. Each body paragraph supports that position.
4. Examples explain a reason rather than replace it.

## Minute two: inspect paragraph logic

Read only the first sentence of each paragraph. Together, these sentences should form a sensible outline of the essay. If two paragraphs begin with the same idea, you may be repeating rather than developing.

Then inspect the connection inside each body paragraph:

- claim;
- explanation;
- specific example or consequence;
- link back to the question.

You do not need to force all four parts into a fixed template, but the reader should understand why the evidence supports the claim.

## Minute three: remove unclear reference words

Circle pronouns and broad reference words such as *this*, *they*, *it*, and *these issues*. Ask what each one points to. If two nouns could be the answer, repeat the precise noun.

Weak: “This causes serious problems.”  
Clearer: “This loss of affordable housing increases commuting time for lower-income workers.”

Also remove linking words that do not match the logic. *However* needs a contrast; *therefore* needs a result; *for example* needs a concrete illustration.

## Minute four: check vocabulary for accuracy

Do not replace common words simply to sound advanced. Check collocations and word forms you are least certain about.

Prioritize:

1. verbs that control meaning, such as *cause*, *allow*, *reduce*, and *require*;
2. countable and uncountable nouns;
3. adjective and adverb forms;
4. repeated words that can be replaced without changing precision.

“Economic” and “economical” are both advanced enough to be dangerous when used incorrectly. Accurate ordinary language is more useful than an impressive mismatch.

## Minute five: run a grammar scan

Read from the final sentence backward. This breaks the flow of the argument and makes sentence-level errors easier to see.

Check one feature at a time:

- subject–verb agreement;
- verb tense;
- articles;
- singular and plural endings;
- sentence boundaries;
- punctuation around dependent clauses.

If a long sentence is difficult to verify, divide it into two correct sentences. Complexity helps only when it remains controlled.

## Worked revision example

**Draft:** “Many cities invest in public transport, this is beneficial because less cars will be used and it also make people to travel cheaper.”

The sentence has a comma splice, a countability error, subject–verb disagreement, and an incorrect verb pattern.

**Revision:** “Many cities invest in public transport because reliable services can reduce car use. They can also make travel cheaper for residents.”

The revision is not more ornate. It is clearer, grammatically controlled, and easier to connect to a specific example.

## Practice the checklist before test day

Write the five categories at the top of your practice page: task, paragraphs, references, vocabulary, grammar. Use the same order after every timed essay. Record only the errors that you actually found.

After four essays, identify the two checks that produce the most corrections. Those become your personal priority. A revision checklist is valuable when it reflects your recurring errors, not when it grows into a list too long to finish.

## What not to do in the final five minutes

Do not rewrite the entire introduction, add a memorized quotation, or replace many words at once. Large late changes create agreement and cohesion errors. Protect the argument first, then correct the most visible language problems.

The goal of revision is not to make the essay look newly written. It is to make the response complete, easy to follow, precise, and controlled.
