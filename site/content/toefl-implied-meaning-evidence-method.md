Implied meaning is not permission to invent a story around a passage. A defensible inference is the smallest conclusion that explains the text without adding outside facts. The two-clue method below makes that boundary visible.

## The two-clue rule

Before choosing an answer, locate two pieces of evidence that work together. One clue establishes the situation; the other establishes direction, cause, contrast, or attitude.

Use this sequence:

1. Restate each clue in plain language.
2. Connect them with a neutral word such as *therefore*, *but*, or *because*.
3. Form the narrowest conclusion the connection supports.
4. Reject any option that adds a new actor, cause, time, or degree.

If you can support an option with only a general feeling about the paragraph, keep looking. The goal is a traceable chain.

## Worked example: a policy change

**Practice text:** The campus café introduced reusable containers in September. By November, orders served in disposable packaging had fallen sharply. The café then extended the return station’s opening hours during final exams.

**Question:** What is most strongly suggested about the return station?

A. Students avoided the café during final exams.  
B. The reusable-container program was receiving enough use to justify wider access.  
C. Disposable packaging was prohibited across the campus.  
D. The café earned more money after September.

Clue one: disposable packaging orders fell after reusable containers were introduced. Clue two: the café expanded access to the return station. Together, they support continued or growing use of the program. **B** is the narrow conclusion. C invents a campus-wide ban; D invents a financial result; A conflicts with the decision to extend access.

## Distinguish implication from possibility

Many statements are possible but not supported. If a passage says a researcher repeated an experiment after a sensor failed, it is possible that the laboratory bought a new sensor. The text does not require that conclusion. The repeat could use a repaired sensor, a borrowed device, or another method.

Test an answer with the “could the text still be true?” question. If the passage could remain completely true while the answer is false, the answer is probably too speculative.

## Watch degree words

Wrong answers often preserve the topic but change the strength. Compare these pairs:

- *may contribute* versus *is the only cause*;
- *some participants* versus *all participants*;
- *less common* versus *never occurs*;
- *the evidence favors* versus *the evidence proves*.

Circle absolute terms in answer choices. An absolute answer can be correct, but it needs absolute evidence.

## Handle attitude without mind-reading

For an attitude question, collect language choices rather than guessing how the author feels. Contrast markers, evaluative adjectives, and the amount of space given to competing views are observable evidence.

If an author calls a proposal “promising” and then spends two paragraphs on unresolved costs, the position is likely cautiously positive, not enthusiastic approval or total rejection. Keep the answer at the same emotional strength as the wording.

## Build a ten-minute drill

Choose one short official or academic-style paragraph and write:

1. two literal facts;
2. one supported inference;
3. one tempting but unsupported inference;
4. the word or sentence that makes the unsupported inference go too far.

This drill trains both selection and rejection. Reviewing why a distractor fails is often more useful than recording only the correct letter.

## Final check

Before submitting an implied-meaning answer, ask:

- Can I point to two clues?
- Did I add a fact that is not present?
- Is my answer stronger than the passage?
- Would the passage still be true if this answer were false?

An inference should feel modest. It closes a small logical gap; it does not build a new paragraph.
