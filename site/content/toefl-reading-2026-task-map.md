The current TOEFL Reading section rewards a broader set of reading behaviors than a single long-passage routine. The official ETS overview groups Reading into three task types: **Complete the Words**, **Read in Daily Life**, and **Read an Academic Passage**. A useful study plan should train each task separately, then combine them under time pressure.

This guide is a planning tool, not a prediction of the questions on a particular test date. Check the official source notes before your exam because formats can change.

## Start with a three-column diagnostic

Complete one official practice set without pausing. For every missed or uncertain answer, record the task family, the reason for the error, and the smallest skill that would have prevented it.

1. Label the task: word completion, daily-life text, or academic passage.
2. Label the failure: vocabulary, sentence grammar, locating a detail, implied meaning, or main idea.
3. Write one repair action that can be repeated in ten minutes.

“Read more carefully” is not a repair action. “Underline the subject and verb before filling a missing word” is. The diagnostic should tell you what to do tomorrow, not merely describe what went wrong today.

## Train Complete the Words as a language puzzle

This task combines local grammar with whole-text meaning. Do not guess from the visible letters alone. First identify what kind of word the sentence needs. A noun, verb, adjective, and adverb occupy different grammatical slots. Then use the surrounding sentence to decide which meaning fits.

Use this short routine:

1. Read the entire sentence before touching the blank.
2. Mark the grammatical role required by the sentence.
3. Read one sentence before and after for topic and tone.
4. Complete the word, then reread the sentence naturally.

For practice, remove several letters from words in a short campus notice or textbook paragraph. The exercise is valuable only if you explain both the grammar clue and the meaning clue for each answer.

## Treat Daily Life texts as information design

Notices, messages, and short informational texts often hide meaning in layout: headings, dates, exceptions, contact details, and conditions. Your first pass should build a map rather than memorize every sentence.

Ask four questions:

- Who is the message for?
- What action is expected?
- When or under what condition does it apply?
- Which sentence contains an exception?

Example: a library notice says that study rooms are available until 9 p.m., **except on public holidays**. A question about Saturday opening cannot be answered from the time alone; the exception controls the answer. Circle words such as *except*, *unless*, *only*, and *before* because they change the rule.

## Read Academic Passages by paragraph function

For an academic passage, give each paragraph a short job label: introduces a problem, explains a mechanism, presents evidence, contrasts a theory, or states a result. These labels create a searchable map.

Suppose three paragraphs do the following:

1. describe a decline in a bird population;
2. compare two possible causes;
3. report evidence supporting habitat loss.

The main point is not simply “birds declined.” The passage explains a decline and evaluates its likely cause. That distinction helps with main-idea and implied-meaning questions because it separates topic from purpose.

## Use a balanced seven-day cycle

A compact weekly plan can expose a weak skill without abandoning the others:

- Day 1: diagnostic set and error labels;
- Day 2: word-completion grammar clues;
- Day 3: daily-life conditions and exceptions;
- Day 4: academic paragraph maps;
- Day 5: implied meaning and evidence;
- Day 6: mixed official practice under time;
- Day 7: review the error log and rewrite three rules.

Repeat the cycle with new material. Increase speed only after your explanations become accurate. A faster guess is still a guess.

## Know when the plan is working

Track decisions, not only scores. Progress is visible when you can name the clue that supports an answer, reject an option with a specific reason, and finish a mixed set without losing accuracy in the final tasks.

At the end of each week, keep one skill, replace one skill, and add one constraint. For example: keep paragraph mapping, replace basic vocabulary review with word-form practice, and add a rule that every inference must cite two textual clues. That produces a study plan that changes with your evidence rather than repeating the same practice indefinitely.
