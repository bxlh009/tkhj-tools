The TOEFL iBT Reading section tests your ability to understand academic texts in English. One of the most frequent question types you will encounter is the **Factual Information** question, often referred to as a "detail" or "fact" question. A closely related variant is the **Negative Factual Information** question, which asks you to identify what is *not* mentioned or true.

These questions account for a significant portion of the reading score. If you are stuck at a score of 24, it often indicates that while you can grasp the general idea of a passage, you are losing points on specific details. You might be missing subtle distinctions between what is stated and what is implied, or you might be spending too much time searching for answers without a systematic approach.

This guide provides a repeatable method for handling these questions. It focuses on precision, evidence-based reasoning, and efficient time management. By mastering this workflow, you can convert uncertainty into accuracy.

## The Core Decision: Locate, Verify, Eliminate

When facing a Factual or Negative Factual question, your primary task is not to interpret the text deeply but to locate specific information and verify its presence or absence. The exact decision you must make is: **"Is this statement explicitly supported by the text, or is it contradicted/absent?"**

For **Factual Information** questions, you must find the option that is directly stated in the passage.
For **Negative Factual Information** questions, you must identify the option that is *not* stated in the passage.

Many test-takers fail because they try to infer meaning or use outside knowledge. The ETS (Educational Testing Service) states that all answers are based solely on the information presented in the passage. Therefore, your strategy must rely entirely on textual evidence.

### Why This Approach Works

1.  **Reduces Cognitive Load:** By focusing only on explicit statements, you avoid overthinking complex sentences.
2.  **Prevents Distractor Traps:** TOEFL questions often include options that sound plausible or use similar vocabulary but change the meaning slightly. A strict verification process exposes these traps.
3.  **Saves Time:** A systematic search is faster than rereading the entire passage for every question.

## Step-by-Step Method for Factual Information Questions

Factual Information questions typically begin with phrases like:
*   "According to paragraph [X], why did...?"
*   "The author mentions [concept] in order to..."
*   "Which of the following is true about [topic]?"

Follow this four-step method:

### Step 1: Identify the Key Term

Read the question stem carefully. Underline or highlight the key term or concept. This is usually a noun, a proper name, or a specific date. For example, if the question asks about "the migration patterns of monarch butterflies," your key term is "migration patterns."

### Step 2: Scan the Passage for the Key Term

Do not read the entire paragraph. Instead, scan the relevant paragraph (indicated in the question, e.g., "According to paragraph 2") for the key term. Look for synonyms or related concepts if the exact word is not present. Academic texts often use varied vocabulary.

### Step 3: Read the Context Sentence

Once you find the sentence containing the key term, read that sentence and the one immediately before and after it. The answer is almost always contained within this three-sentence window. Pay attention to modifiers (words like *only*, *all*, *some*, *never*) as they often distinguish correct answers from distractors.

### Step 4: Compare and Eliminate Options

Compare each of the four answer choices against the context sentences.
*   **Correct Answer:** Paraphrases the information in the text accurately.
*   **Distractor 1 (Opposite):** States the opposite of what the text says.
*   **Distractor 2 (Not Mentioned):** Contains words from the text but combines them in a way that is not supported by the passage.
*   **Distractor 3 (Too Broad/Specific):** Generalizes a specific point or makes a claim that is too narrow to be the main answer.

## Step-by-Step Method for Negative Factual Information Questions

Negative Factual Information questions ask you to identify what is **NOT** true or **NOT** mentioned. These questions typically start with:
*   "All of the following are mentioned in paragraph [X] EXCEPT..."
*   "Which of the following is NOT true about [topic]?"

The method is similar but requires a different mindset: you are looking for the outlier.

### Step 1: Identify the Subject

Determine the main subject of the question. For example, if the question is about "characteristics of volcanic eruptions," focus on that topic.

### Step 2: Scan and Verify Three Options

Scan the relevant paragraph for the details listed in the answer choices. You need to find evidence for **three** of the four options. Since only one option is incorrect (or not mentioned), finding evidence for three choices automatically identifies the fourth as the answer.

### Step 3: Confirm the Exception

If you have found evidence for three options, the remaining option is your answer. However, double-check that the third option you verified is indeed in the text. Sometimes, two options might seem plausible, so ensure you are strictly adhering to the text.

## Common Pitfalls and Boundaries

To improve your score from 24, you must avoid these common errors:

### 1. Using Outside Knowledge
You may know that volcanoes are dangerous. However, if the passage does not mention danger, you cannot choose an answer based on that fact. Stick strictly to the text.

### 2. Confusing "Main Idea" with "Detail"
Factual questions ask for specific details, not the main idea. Do not choose an answer that summarizes the whole paragraph unless the question specifically asks for the main purpose.

### 3. Ignoring Modifiers
Words like *always*, *never*, *often*, and *sometimes* are critical. If the text says "often," an answer choice saying "always" is incorrect.

### 4. Spending Too Much Time
If you cannot find the answer within 60–90 seconds, guess and move on. Revisiting later wastes valuable time needed for other sections.

## Original Practice Example 1: Factual Information

**Passage Excerpt: The Evolution of Urban Planning**

Urban planning emerged as a distinct profession in the late 19th century, driven by the rapid industrialization of cities. Prior to this period, city growth was largely unregulated, leading to overcrowded living conditions and poor sanitation. The first comprehensive zoning laws were introduced in New York City in 1916. These laws divided the city into districts where specific types of buildings could be constructed. For instance, residential areas were separated from industrial zones to protect residents from noise and pollution. This separation was not merely aesthetic; it was a public health measure. Early planners believed that separating incompatible land uses would reduce the spread of disease and improve the overall quality of life for urban dwellers. While modern urban planning has evolved to include more complex considerations such as sustainability and social equity, the fundamental principle of zoning remains a cornerstone of city design.

**Question 1:** According to paragraph 1, why were the first zoning laws introduced in New York City?

A. To increase the aesthetic appeal of residential neighborhoods.
B. To separate incompatible land uses for public health reasons.
C. To promote social equity among different economic classes.
D. To encourage the development of new industrial technologies.

**Answer and Evidence-Based Reasoning:**

**Correct Answer: B**

**Reasoning:**
1.  **Identify Key Term:** "why were the first zoning laws introduced"
2.  **Locate in Text:** The text states, "This separation was not merely aesthetic; it was a public health measure." It further explains, "Early planners believed that separating incompatible land uses would reduce the spread of disease..."
3.  **Evaluate Options:**
    *   A is incorrect because the text explicitly says the separation was "not merely aesthetic."
    *   B is correct because it matches the text's explanation of "public health measure" and reducing disease.
    *   C is incorrect because "social equity" is mentioned as a *modern* consideration, not a reason for the *first* laws.
    *   D is incorrect because the text does not mention promoting industrial technologies.

***

**Question 2:** Which of the following is NOT mentioned in paragraph 1 as a characteristic of cities prior to the late 19th century?

A. Rapid growth due to industrialization.
B. Lack of regulation regarding city expansion.
C. Overcrowded living conditions.
D. Advanced sanitation systems.

**Answer and Evidence-Based Reasoning:**

**Correct Answer: D**

**Reasoning:**
1.  **Identify Key Term:** "NOT mentioned... prior to the late 19th century"
2.  **Scan for Details:**
    *   The text mentions "rapid industrialization" (supports A).
    *   The text mentions "city growth was largely unregulated" (supports B).
    *   The text mentions "overcrowded living conditions" (supports C).
    *   The text mentions "poor sanitation," which implies the *lack* of advanced systems.
3.  **Evaluate Options:**
    *   A, B, and C are explicitly stated in the text.
    *   D is the exception. The text states there was "poor sanitation," so "advanced sanitation systems" were not present. Therefore, D is the correct answer for a negative factual question.

## Original Practice Example 2: Negative Factual Information

*Note: These examples are non-official and intended for practice only.*

**Passage Excerpt: The Role of Mycorrhizal Fungi**

Mycorrhizal fungi form symbiotic relationships with the roots of most terrestrial plants. In this partnership, the fungi extend the root system of the plant, allowing it to absorb water and nutrients more efficiently. In return, the plant provides the fungi with carbohydrates produced through photosynthesis. This relationship is particularly crucial in nutrient-poor soils, where the fungi help extract phosphorus and nitrogen that the plant roots cannot reach alone. Research has shown that mycorrhizal networks can connect multiple plants, facilitating the transfer of resources between them. Some studies suggest that older trees may share nutrients with younger saplings through these fungal networks, enhancing the survival rate of the forest ecosystem. However, not all plants benefit equally from this association; some species, such as those in the Brassicaceae family, do not form mycorrhizal relationships.

**Question 3:** All of the following are benefits of mycorrhizal fungi to plants EXCEPT:

A. Increased efficiency in water absorption.
B. Access to nutrients in nutrient-poor soils.
C. Production of carbohydrates through photosynthesis.
D. Enhanced survival rates of forest ecosystems.

**Answer and Evidence-Based Reasoning:**

**Correct Answer: C**

**Reasoning:**
1.  **Identify Key Term:** "benefits... EXCEPT"
2.  **Scan for Benefits:**
    *   The text says fungi allow plants to "absorb water and nutrients more efficiently" (supports A).
    *   The text mentions helping extract phosphorus and nitrogen in "nutrient-poor soils" (supports B).
    *   The text notes that the relationship enhances the "survival rate of the forest ecosystem" (supports D).
3.  **Evaluate Options:**
    *   A, B, and D are all described as benefits provided by the fungi.
    *   C is incorrect because the text states the *plant* provides carbohydrates to the fungi, not the other way around. The plant produces carbohydrates via photosynthesis independently. Therefore, C is the exception.

***

**Question 4:** According to the passage, which of the following is true about the Brassicaceae family?

A. They rely heavily on mycorrhizal networks for survival.
B. They do not form mycorrhizal relationships.
C. They provide carbohydrates to older trees.
D. They thrive best in nutrient-rich soils.

**Answer and Evidence-Based Reasoning:**

**Correct Answer: B**

**Reasoning:**
1.  **Identify Key Term:** "Brassicaceae family"
2.  **Locate in Text:** The last sentence states, "...some species, such as those in the Brassicaceae family, do not form mycorrhizal relationships."
3.  **Evaluate Options:**
    *   A is incorrect because they do not form the relationships.
    *   B is correct because it directly paraphrases the text.
    *   C is incorrect because the text does not mention this family providing carbohydrates.
    *   D is incorrect because soil preference is not discussed for this specific family.

## Strategic Application for Score Improvement

To move from a 24 to a higher score, you must apply these methods consistently. Here is how to integrate them into your study routine:

1.  **Practice Active Scanning:** When reading practice passages, do not just read passively. Actively look for keywords and signal words (e.g., *however*, *therefore*, *for example*). This trains your eyes to locate information quickly.
2.  **Analyze Distractors:** After completing practice questions, review every wrong answer. Understand *why* it was wrong. Was it a detail error? A scope error? This analysis is more valuable than simply getting the right answer.
3.  **Time Management Drill:** Set a timer for 18 minutes per passage. Within that time, you should answer 10–12 questions. If you finish early, use the remaining time to review. If you run out of time, identify which question types took too long and practice those specifically.
4.  **Vocabulary Building:** Many factual questions hinge on understanding specific academic vocabulary. Keep a list of words you encounter in practice passages and review them regularly.

## Conclusion

Fact and Negative Fact questions are designed to test your reading precision, not your intuition. By adopting a structured approach—identifying key terms, scanning for evidence, and rigorously verifying options—you can minimize errors and save time. Remember that the TOEFL Reading section rewards careful attention to detail. Practice these methods until they become automatic, and you will see a measurable improvement in your accuracy and confidence.
