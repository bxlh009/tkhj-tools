**The Right Way: Learning Collocations**

Collocations are word partnerships that native speakers use automatically. Some are arbitrary. You just have to memorize them. `Make a decision`, not `do a decision`. `Commit a crime`, not `make a crime`. Other collocations are predictable based on meaning. `Strong argument`, `heavy rain`, `strict regulations`.

For TOEFL, the most important collocations fall into these categories:

1. Verb + preposition combinations: `rely on`, `consist of`, `lead to`, `result from`
2. Adjective + noun pairings: `inherent risk`, `significant impact`, `gradual change`
3. Noun + verb partnerships: `evidence suggest`, `data indicate`, `theory propose`
4. Fixed academic phrases: `on the contrary`, `as a result`, `in terms of`

When you learn these chunks, you do not process each word separately. Your brain recognizes the pattern instantly. Reading becomes faster. Comprehension improves. Vocabulary questions become easier because you understand how words function in real sentences.

Here is the practical method.

**Step 1: Identify High-Frequency Collocations**

Do not try to learn all 800 collocations at once. That is overwhelming. Start with the most common ones. Focus on verb-preposition combinations and adjective-noun pairings. These appear constantly in TOEFL reading passages.

Use the `toefl academic word list` as your foundation. This list contains 570 word families that appear frequently in academic texts. But do not study individual words. Study how those words combine with others.

**Step 2: Learn in Context**



Don't ever learn a collocation in isolation—seriously, you shouldn't. You're way better off seeing it in a sentence, or here's the thing—a whole paragraph. Your brain can't lock in meaning without context, and it hasn't got a clue how to do it solo.

Instead of writing `derive = come from`, write:
"`derive from` = come from; originate in"
Example: "Many diseases derive from environmental factors."

This takes slightly longer. But it sticks better. And it transfers to reading comprehension.

**Step 3: Practice Recognition, Not Production**

TOEFL reading tests recognition. You do not need to produce collocations in speaking or writing (though that helps). You need to spot them quickly when reading.

Practice by reading academic passages and highlighting collocations. Do not stop to look up every unknown word. Just notice the patterns. Which verbs commonly pair with which prepositions? Which adjectives modify which nouns?

**Step 4: Use Spaced Repetition Properly**

Flashcards still work. But not for isolated definitions. Use them for collocations in context.

Front: `_____ from` (meaning: come from, originate in)
Back: `derive from`; Example sentence from a passage you read

This forces your brain to retrieve the collocation, not just the definition. Much stronger learning.

**Three More Mini Comparisons**

Let me walk you through three more situations where the normal advice fails and the collocation method succeeds.

Comparison 1: Understanding `subsequent`

Normal advice: Memorize `subsequent` means "following." Read a sentence. Struggle to understand the temporal relationship.

Collocation method: Learn `subsequent to` as a phrase meaning "after." Notice it appears frequently in historical passages. "Subsequent to the revolution, the government implemented reforms." Instant understanding. Clear temporal marker.

Comparison 2: Decoding `inherent`

Normal advice: Memorize `inherent` means "existing in something as a basic part." Confusing. Abstract. Hard to apply.

Collocation method: Learn `inherent risk`, `inherent limitation`, `inherent advantage`. These are fixed pairings. When you see `inherent`, expect a noun describing a quality or characteristic. "The inherent risk of this procedure is infection." Makes sense immediately.

Comparison 3: Parsing `consist of`

Normal advice: Memorize `consist` means "be made up of." Forget the preposition. See `consist from` in a passage. Panic. Think you do not know the word.

Collocation method: Learn `consist of` as a single unit. Never `consist from`. Never `consist with`. Fixed preposition. Instant recognition. "The committee consists of five members." Done.

**The 800 Collocations Framework**

You do not need to memorize all 800 collocations before the test. But you do need a systematic approach. Here is what I recommend.

First, categorize collocations by type. Verb-preposition combinations get their own section. Adjective-noun pairings get another. Fixed academic phrases get a third. This prevents confusion.

Second, prioritize by frequency. Some collocations appear in almost every reading passage. `Consist of`, `result from`, `significant impact`, `inherent risk`. Learn these first. They give you the highest return on investment.

Third, practice in authentic contexts. Read academic articles. Notice which collocations appear repeatedly. The `toefl vocabulary 800 collocations` list is useful, but real passages teach you better. They show you how collocations function in actual discourse.

Fourth, test yourself actively. Do not just reread your notes. Cover the meaning. Try to recall the collocation. If you cannot remember, look it up and write a new example sentence. Active retrieval strengthens memory far more than passive review.
