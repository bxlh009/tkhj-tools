Scoring a 22 on the TOEFL iBT Reading section is a common plateau. At this level, test-takers typically possess strong vocabulary and can locate explicit information quickly. However, they often lose points on inference questions because they treat every question as a "find-the-answer" task rather than a logic puzzle. The difference between a 22 and a 30+ is not just knowing more words; it is knowing how to deduce meaning that is not explicitly stated in the text.

This article provides a repeatable method for handling inference questions. It focuses on the specific decision you must make during the exam: **Do I have enough evidence in the text to support a logical deduction, or am I guessing based on outside knowledge?**

## The Core Decision: Explicit vs. Implicit

The ETS (Educational Testing Service) defines the TOEFL iBT Reading section as assessing the ability to understand academic texts in English. The content covers natural sciences, arts, history, and social sciences. The questions are categorized into several types, including Factual Information, Negative Factual Information, Vocabulary, Reference, Sentence Simplification, Insert Text, Prose Summary, and **Inference** (often labeled as "Negative Factual," "Sentence Simplification," or specifically "Inference" depending on the test version).

For the purpose of this strategy, we focus on **Inference Questions**. These questions ask you to draw a conclusion that is not directly stated but is strongly supported by the text.

**The Decision Point:**
When you encounter an inference question, you must decide if the answer is:
1.  **Directly Supported:** The text provides premises A and B, and the answer is C, which logically follows from A and B.
2.  **Outside Knowledge:** The answer seems true in the real world but is not mentioned or implied in the passage.
3.  **Too Strong/Weak:** The answer uses absolute language (all, never, always) or vague language (some, might) that does not match the precision of the text.

If you cannot find direct textual support for the logical link, you must reject the option. This discipline prevents the "I know this fact" trap, which is the primary reason scorers stall at 22.

## A Repeatable Method: The Evidence-Link-Deduce Framework

To manage time effectively and improve accuracy, use the following three-step framework. This method replaces reading every word with intense scrutiny with a targeted search for logical connectors.

### Step 1: Identify the Question Type and Target Location

Inference questions usually reference a specific sentence or paragraph. Do not scan the entire passage. Go directly to the cited location.

*   **Why this works:** It saves time. Most errors come from misreading the context or bringing in irrelevant information from other parts of the text.
*   **Action:** Read the question stem first. Note the key terms (names, dates, concepts). Locate these terms in the passage.

### Step 2: Extract the Premises

Read the surrounding sentences carefully. Identify the facts presented. Look for causal links (because, therefore, as a result), contrasts (however, although), or comparisons (similarly, unlike).

*   **Why this works:** Inference is built on premises. If the text says "X causes Y," and the question asks about X, you need to know what the text says about Y.
*   **Action:** Mentally or physically underline the core claim. Ignore adjectives and examples unless they are crucial to the definition.

### Step 3: Deduce and Match

Formulate your own prediction before looking at the options. Ask yourself: "What *must* be true if the text is true?" Then, compare this prediction to the choices.

*   **Why this works:** It prevents you from being swayed by plausible-sounding distractors. Distractors often use words from the text but change their relationship or intensity.
*   **Action:** Eliminate options that:
    *   Are not mentioned in the text.
    *   Contradict the text.
    *   Are too extreme (e.g., the text says "often," the option says "always").
    *   Rely on general knowledge rather than the specific argument of the passage.

## Time Management for Inference Questions

Time management is critical. A typical TOEFL Reading section has 3–4 passages and 10 questions per passage, with 54–72 minutes total. This averages to about 13–18 minutes per passage.

Inference questions take longer than factual questions because they require processing logic, not just scanning. To manage this:

1.  **Flag and Move:** If you spend more than 90 seconds on an inference question without finding clear evidence, mark it and move on. Return to it if time permits.
2.  **Prioritize Accuracy Over Speed in Logic:** It is better to spend 2 minutes on one hard inference question correctly than to rush through three and get them wrong.
3.  **Use Process of Elimination (POE):** Often, you don’t need to find the right answer immediately; you need to eliminate the four wrong ones. Look for "red flags" like absolute words or unsupported comparisons.

## Original Practice Example 1: Biological Adaptation

*Note: The following passage and questions are non-official practice material created for educational purposes. They are not derived from any official ETS test.*

**Passage Excerpt:**
> "Cacti in arid environments have evolved specialized mechanisms to conserve water. Unlike most plants, which open their stomata during the day to take in carbon dioxide for photosynthesis, many cacti open their stomata only at night. This adaptation, known as Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM), allows the plant to minimize water loss through evaporation during the hottest part of the day. During the night, when temperatures are lower and humidity is higher, the cactus absorbs CO2 and stores it as malic acid. During the day, the stored CO2 is released for use in photosynthesis while the stomata remain closed."

**Question 1:**
Based on the passage, why do cacti store CO2 as malic acid at night?

A. Malic acid is more efficient for photosynthesis than raw CO2.
B. The process of converting CO2 to malic acid requires low temperatures.
C. It allows the cactus to perform photosynthesis during the day while keeping stomata closed.
D. Cacti cannot absorb CO2 during the day because the air is too dry.

**Answer and Reasoning:**

**Correct Answer: C**

**Evidence-Based Reasoning:**
1.  **Locate Evidence:** The text states, "During the night... the cactus absorbs CO2 and stores it as malic acid." It then states, "During the day, the stored CO2 is released for use in photosynthesis while the stomata remain closed."
2.  **Identify the Link:** The storage of CO2 as malic acid at night is directly linked to the ability to release it for photosynthesis during the day *while the stomata are closed*.
3.  **Evaluate Options:**
    *   **A:** The text does not compare the efficiency of malic acid vs. raw CO2. It only explains the timing mechanism.
    *   **B:** The text mentions low temperatures help minimize water loss, but it does not state that the chemical conversion *requires* low temperatures. It says absorption happens at night *when* temperatures are lower.
    *   **C:** This matches the logical deduction. The storage enables daytime photosynthesis with closed stomata.
    *   **D:** While the text implies daytime absorption would lead to water loss, it does not state that cacti *cannot* absorb CO2 during the day due to dryness alone, but rather that they *choose* not to open stomata to conserve water. Option C is the stronger, more direct inference based on the described mechanism.

**Common Misapplication:**
Test-takers often choose D because it sounds biologically plausible. However, the text focuses on the *mechanism* of CAM (storing acid to use later), not just the inability to breathe during the day. The inference must be strictly tied to the function described: storage enables delayed usage.

## Original Practice Example 2: Historical Economic Shift

*Note: The following passage and questions are non-official practice material created for educational purposes. They are not derived from any official ETS test.*

**Passage Excerpt:**
> "The transition from agrarian societies to industrial ones in 19th-century Europe was not merely a change in technology but a profound shift in social structure. As factories replaced home-based production, the family unit underwent significant transformation. In agrarian economies, family members worked together on land, sharing both labor and profits. In industrial settings, wage labor became individualized. Men, women, and children often worked in separate factories or different departments within the same factory. This separation led to a new dynamic where income was no longer a collective family resource but often controlled by individual earners, altering traditional power dynamics within the household."

**Question 2:**
It can be inferred from the passage that in pre-industrial agrarian societies, the economic value of a child’s labor was likely viewed differently than in industrial societies because:

A. Children in agrarian societies did not work as much as those in industrial societies.
B. The labor of all family members contributed to a shared family enterprise rather than individual wages.
C. Industrial laws prohibited children from working in factories until they reached adulthood.
D. Agrarian families were wealthier than industrial families, so children did not need to contribute financially.

**Answer and Reasoning:**

**Correct Answer: B**

**Evidence-Based Reasoning:**
1.  **Locate Evidence:** The text contrasts agrarian and industrial structures. For agrarian: "family members worked together on land, sharing both labor and profits." For industrial: "wage labor became individualized... income was no longer a collective family resource but often controlled by individual earners."
2.  **Identify the Link:** The difference in view of labor stems from the structure of ownership and profit distribution. In agrarian settings, labor was part of a "shared family enterprise."
3.  **Evaluate Options:**
    *   **A:** The text does not compare the *amount* of work, but the *structure* of the work.
    *   **B:** This directly infers from "sharing both labor and profits" vs. "individualized" wage labor. The value was collective, not individual.
    *   **C:** The text mentions children worked in separate factories, implying they *did* work. It does not mention legal prohibitions.
    *   **D:** The text does not discuss the relative wealth of the two groups, only the structure of their labor and income.

**Common Misapplication:**
Test-takers may choose A because they assume industrialization increased child labor hours. While historically true in some contexts, the *passage* does not provide data on hours worked. It provides data on *how* labor was organized and compensated. The inference must be limited to the provided text.

## Limits and Common Misapplications

Even with a solid framework, inference questions have limits. Understanding these boundaries is crucial for maintaining a score above 22.

### 1. The "Too Strong" Trap
ETS frequently uses absolute language in incorrect answers. Words like *always, never, all, none, must, impossible* are red flags. Unless the text explicitly supports an absolute claim, the correct answer will usually use softer language like *may, might, likely, often, some*.
*   **Strategy:** If an answer choice feels too definitive, check the text for qualifiers. If the text says "many scientists believe," an answer saying "it is proven" is incorrect.

### 2. The "Outside Knowledge" Trap
This is the most common error for high-intermediate learners. You might know that "photosynthesis requires sunlight." If the passage doesn't mention sunlight, you cannot infer it. The TOEFL tests reading comprehension, not general knowledge.
*   **Strategy:** Treat the passage as a self-contained universe. If it's not in the text, it doesn't exist for the purpose of the question.

### 3. The "Partial Truth" Trap
Some answers contain a true statement from the text but do not answer the specific inference question. They might be relevant to the topic but irrelevant to the logical deduction requested.
*   **Strategy:** Always re-read the question stem. Does the answer address the *why*, *how*, or *what can be concluded*? Or does it just state a fact?

### 4. Ignoring Contextual Clues
Inference often relies on tone or contrast. Words like *however, although, despite* signal that the author is presenting a counter-point. Missing these signals leads to misinterpreting the author's intent.
*   **Strategy:** Pay attention to transitional phrases. They often hold the key to the logical relationship between ideas.

## Conclusion: Moving Beyond 22

Reaching a 22 in TOEFL Reading indicates a strong foundation. To break through to 26+, 30+, or higher, you must shift from passive reading to active logical analysis. The key is not to read faster, but to think deeper about the connections between sentences.

By using the Evidence-Link-Deduce framework, you create a disciplined approach to inference questions. You stop guessing and start deriving. You stop relying on what you know and start focusing on what the text says. This shift in mindset, combined with strict time management, is the most effective way to improve your score.

Remember: Every inference must be anchored in the text. If you cannot point to the specific words that support your conclusion, your answer is likely incorrect. Practice this discipline with every question, and you will see a measurable improvement in your accuracy and confidence.
