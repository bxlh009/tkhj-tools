1. Summarizing the whole passage instead of the paragraph scope
What it looks like: The question asks for the main idea of paragraph three, but you scan the introduction and conclusion first. You pick the option that covers the entire essay.
Why it happens: Your brain wants efficiency. You assume the big picture always wins. Test makers exploit that assumption by giving you a perfectly accurate summary of the whole text as a distractor.
The fix: Circle the paragraph number in the question stem. Read only that paragraph. If the question does not specify a paragraph, look at the first and last sentences of the opening section. I know this sounds too simple. Trust me, I thought so too. Sophie went from R19 to R27 in four weeks by ditching full reading and skimming first sentences only. She stopped chasing the macro theme and started respecting the micro scope.

2. Falling for extreme language traps
What it looks like: You see options with words like always, never, completely, or exclusively. You eliminate them immediately because you were taught absolutes are wrong.
Why it happens: Standardized tests do punish extreme language, but not universally. Sometimes the correct answer genuinely describes a definitive shift or a strict scientific boundary.
The fix: Treat extremes as red flags, not automatic eliminations. Check the text. Does the author use absolute phrasing to describe a law, a definition, or a proven result? If yes, keep it. If the text uses hedging like suggests, indicates, or often, then discard the extreme option. I spent three weeks memorizing GRE vocabulary before realizing most high-frequency words overlap with general reading. The same applies here. Context dictates the strength of the claim.

3. Confusing detail with main idea
What it looks like: An option repeats a specific statistic, name, or example from the passage. It is factually correct, but it is just a brick in the wall, not the wall itself.
Why it happens: Details are easy to spot. They anchor your attention. When you read actively, your brain highlights concrete nouns and numbers. You mistake recognition for comprehension.
The fix: Ask yourself what the detail proves. Every piece of evidence exists to support a broader claim. Strip away the example. What remains is the main idea. When I took my first full practice test, I ate a heavy breakfast and spent twenty minutes analyzing a single graph in the second paragraph. I panicked when the clock hit forty-five minutes. I recovered by forcing myself to answer the next three questions in under two minutes each. Speed forced me to stop worshipping details.

4. Overthinking author intent beyond the text
What it looks like: You guess what the writer feels, hopes, or believes about life. You project your own worldview onto a dry academic paragraph.
Why it happens: We are trained to read literature for emotion and subtext. TOEFL reading is not literature. It is academic reporting. The author does not care about your feelings. They care about logical structure.
The fix: Replace emotional guessing with functional analysis. Instead of asking what the author wants you to feel, ask what the author wants you to understand. Purpose questions demand this shift. I helped a student named Jake who was stuck at W6.0 on writing. He dropped his thesaurus and started writing like he speaks. The same principle applies here. Stop performing. Just report what the text does.

5. Ignoring transition words that signal shift in focus
What it looks like: You read straight through a paragraph without pausing at however, therefore, consequently, or in contrast. You miss the pivot.
Why it happens: Transition markers feel like filler. You skim past them to get to the meat. But the meat changes direction at those exact words.
The fix: Train your eyes to track logical connectors. When you see a contrast word, expect the main idea to flip or narrow. When you see a cause-effect word, expect the paragraph to move from problem to solution. Tom improved his listening score from L24 to L29 by stopping translation in his head and listening for structure instead of words. Reading works the same way. Track the architecture, not the bricks.

6. Picking the answer that sounds smart but lacks textual support
What it looks like: One option uses sophisticated academic phrasing. It sounds like something a professor would say. You choose it because it feels authoritative.
Why it happens: We associate complexity with correctness. It is a cognitive bias. The test deliberately rewards precise, plain-language summaries over flashy paraphrases.
The fix: Favor clarity over cleverness. The correct main idea usually mirrors the paragraph's tone and complexity level. If the passage discusses a straightforward historical trend, the answer will not suddenly introduce quantum mechanics metaphors. Keep it grounded.

7. Rushing through the question stem before reading options
What it looks like: You glance at the question, see the words main idea, and immediately hunt for a summary. You miss qualifiers like primarily, initially, or ultimately.
Why it happens: Time pressure makes you skim stems. You assume all main idea questions are identical. They are not.
The fix: Read the stem twice. Highlight the qualifier. If it says primarily, you are looking for the dominant point. If it says initially, you are looking for the opening premise. Priyanka hit a hard plateau at Q162 on the GRE. She realized she was double-checking every answer. She forced a move-on rule. Apply that same discipline here. If the stem changes the focus, adjust your reading lens immediately.

8. Letting prior knowledge override the passage
What it looks like: You know climate change causes ice melt. The passage says ice melt causes ocean currents to slow. You ignore the second claim because it contradicts your textbook.
Why it happens: You bring outside expertise into a room that only cares about the text. The TOEFL does not grade your general knowledge. It grades your ability to extract information from the provided material.
The fix: Treat the passage as the only universe that exists. Even if the text claims birds migrate south in winter, you accept it. I remember staring at a biology passage that described a plant thriving in desert conditions. My biology background screamed that plants need water. I almost marked it wrong. I caught myself, re-read the sentence, and saw it mentioned a specialized root system. I learned to suspend my ego at the door.

9. Misreading purpose versus main idea distinctions
What it looks like: You treat purpose questions and main idea questions as interchangeable. You give the same answer to both.
Why it happens: Both ask for the core message. But purpose asks why the author wrote it. Main idea asks what the author said.
The fix: Purpose answers start with action verbs. The author explains, contrasts, challenges, or illustrates. Main idea answers state facts or themes. The rock contains iron. Fact. The geologist alleges. Allegation. When you see purpose questions, look for function. When you see main idea questions, look for content. Aisha improved her speaking score from S6.5 to S8.0 by throwing out memorized scripts and having real conversations. Stop reciting templates. Match the verb to the question type.

10. Second-guessing your first instinct without evidence
What it looks like: You pick A. You doubt it. You scan B. It looks slightly better. You switch. You get it wrong.
Why it happens: Anxiety triggers revision loops. You lose trust in your initial processing.
The fix: Only change your answer if you find a direct contradiction in the text. If you cannot point to a sentence that invalidates your choice, leave it alone. I know this sounds too simple. Trust me, I thought so too. Your first read captures the gist. Your second read introduces noise. Protect your initial extraction.

Worked Example 1: Paragraph Main Idea Focus

The following passage describes how early agricultural societies adapted to seasonal flooding in river valleys. Communities developed irrigation channels to divert water during peak melt periods. These channels allowed crops to grow even when rainfall was scarce. Over generations, farmers refined their techniques, building larger storage facilities to preserve surplus grain. The surplus supported population growth and enabled specialization in crafts and trade. Eventually, these settlements evolved into complex urban centers with organized governance.

Question: What is the main idea of the paragraph?
A) Early farmers struggled to survive unpredictable weather patterns.
B) Irrigation systems directly caused the rise of urban civilizations.
C) Agricultural adaptations to flooding created food surpluses that enabled societal development.
D) River valleys were the only locations where early humans could establish permanent settlements.

Solution: Step 1: Identify scope. The paragraph traces a chain of events starting with flooding adaptation, moving to irrigation, then surplus storage, population growth, and finally urban centers. Step 2: Eliminate extremes and unsupported claims. Option A focuses on struggle, but the text emphasizes adaptation and success. Option D uses only, which is extreme and unsupported. Step 3: Match function to content. Option B jumps straight to urbanization, skipping the intermediate steps. Option C captures the causal chain: adaptation → surplus → development. The correct answer is C.

Pitfall Summary: Students often pick B because urbanization is the final outcome. But main idea questions reward the complete logical arc, not just the endpoint. I wasted months picking endpoints until I started tracking the middle steps.

Worked Example 2: Author Purpose Focus

Historians once believed that the collapse of the Bronze Age civilizations resulted solely from massive seismic activity. Recent archaeological surveys, however, reveal widespread evidence of prolonged drought across the eastern Mediterranean. Pollen samples indicate a sharp decline in vegetation between 1200 and 1150 BCE. While earthquakes certainly occurred, the climatic data suggests that resource scarcity weakened trade networks and triggered social fragmentation.

Question: What is the author's primary purpose in writing this paragraph?
A) To argue that earthquakes were completely harmless to ancient societies.
B) To compare the severity of droughts versus earthquakes in different regions.
C) To challenge a traditional explanation by introducing new environmental evidence.
D) To persuade readers to fund additional geological research in the Mediterranean.

Solution: Step 1: Locate the pivot. The word however signals a shift from old belief to new evidence. Step 2: Identify the contrast. Traditional view = earthquakes caused collapse. New evidence = drought and resource scarcity played a major role. Step 3: Match purpose verbs. Option A uses completely, which is extreme and contradicted by the text. Option B focuses on comparison, but the paragraph does not weigh severity. Option D introduces funding, which is absent. Option C correctly identifies the function: challenging an old theory with new data. The correct answer is C.

Pitfall Summary: Readers often miss that purpose questions require action-oriented answers. You are not summarizing facts. You are identifying what the author is doing with those facts. I used to summarize instead of function-reading until I started labeling every paragraph with a single verb like explain, contrast, or revise.
