At the 2026 Shell Finance Annual Conference entrepreneur roadshow in Beijing, Yang Jinsong, founder and CEO of Beijing FutureSense Intelligence Technology (AutoAgents), made a claim that cuts through most of the AI hype. The real measure of AI value, he told the audience, is no longer whether an answer sounds fluent. It is whether the system can enter your production line, access your tools, follow your permissions, and execute tasks. The company core product is a platform called Lingda.

### What Lingda Actually Does

Lingda positions itself as an enterprise AI agent platform. The core pitch: it connects to existing business systems and integrates internal knowledge assets, then lets AI agents execute real workflows with tool-calling and business-process capabilities. That positioning is meaningful because right now, plenty of AI products stop at chat. You ask a question, you get a reply, and then you still have to do the actual work yourself. Lingda is going one step further: the agent should be doing the work, not just talking about it.

The company primary clients sound like lineup from a Chinese industry who is who: State Grid, Sinopec, PetroChina, and ChemChina. These are not startups testing a demo. These are massive state-owned enterprises with compliance requirements, legacy systems, and zero tolerance for hallucinations. When a company like PetroChina signs on as a customer, it means the product cleared procurement review, data compliance checks, and likely a pilot period. That bar exists in a different universe from internet startup signups.

### The Use Case That Matters

Here is where it gets concrete. FutureSense built a digital worker for State Grid that handles transformer equipment fault detection and analysis. According to the company, the system automatically generates maintenance assessment reports. The time savings they cite are significant: tasks that previously took experienced specialists approximately one week are reportedly handled in minutes. The company currently has 35 digital job roles in the power grid sector, covering frontline power supply services, equipment maintenance, compliance audits, and emergency response.

Let me pause here and acknowledge what you might be thinking. Claims like "week reduced to minutes" are easy to make in a press release. I do not have independent verification or audited benchmarks to back up that specific number. FutureSense has not published third-party test reports. So treat that as the company stated figure, not a independently confirmed result. What I can verify is the customer list. State Grid and PetroChina are public companies, and their procurement decisions are documented. If FutureSense named them in a public forum, the contract relationship is real.

### The Marketplace Play: Profy

FutureSense also launched a marketplace called Profy. The explicit ambition: become "the JD.com of digital labor." For small and medium businesses that cannot build internal AI teams, the idea is to purchase pre-trained agents directly from the marketplace and deploy them. This is the part I find most interesting because it addresses a real gap. Most SME owners do not want to fine-tune an LLM. They want an agent that can handle invoicing, scheduling, or customer triage, and they want it working by Monday.

Training these agents relies on a different kind of data than the standard Q&A pairs used for most LLMs. According to FutureSense, the agents learn from operation trajectories: task planning paths, tool-calling patterns, problem-solving processes, and user feedback. If they are actually capturing that cycle, the agent quality should improve with use. Whether they are doing it well enough to matter is the open question.

### Where They Are Heading

Yang Jinsong said the company is pushing agents from L3 ("actor" level, executing assigned tasks) toward L4 ("explorer" level, assisting with research and discovery). They are working with Peking University and Shandong University on AI for Science projects, including controlled nuclear fusion research. That is a long bet. Most companies at this stage stick to narrowing the gap between demo and production. Trying to reach L4 at the same time is either ambition or distraction, and the next twelve months should tell us which.

### The Competitive Landscape

FutureSense is not operating in a vacuum. The enterprise AI agent space is getting crowded. Competitioners include established platforms like Coze, Dify, and international players like CrewAI and AutoGen. What differentiates Lingda appears to be the depth of vertical integration in the power grid industry and the direct enterprise channel. Coze and Dify are stronger on developer ecosystem and broader consumer reach. FutureSense has chosen to go deep on specific industries rather than wide across all of them. That is a viable strategy, but it caps the total addressable market more quickly. If the power grid use case holds and scales, the model could expand to other energy and infrastructure sectors. If it does not, the company remains niche.

### Bottom Line

Lingda is a real product with real clients in the most conservative industries you can name. That alone separates it from 90% of AI agent pitches. What remains unproven is whether the marketplace model for agent distribution actually works at scale, and whether the L4 research pivot dilutes focus or multiplies value. For now, FutureSense is executing on a real trajectory, and if you are tracking where enterprise AI agents land in industry-not-demo contexts, this is one to include on your list. Watch for three signals over the next six months: whether the customer list expands beyond energy, whether the Profy marketplace shows real transaction volume, and whether the L4 research produces anything that survives peer review.
