Apple filed a lawsuit against OpenAI in US federal court in July 2026, alleging a "coordinated campaign" to steal trade secrets through employee recruitment. The complaint names OpenAI chief hardware officer Tang Tan, engineer Chang Liu, and IO Products, the AI hardware startup founded by Tan that OpenAI acquired. What makes this lawsuit unusual is not that companies sue each other over employee mobility. Trade secret litigation happens regularly in technology. What makes it unusual is the specificity of the allegations, the seniority of the people involved, and the size of the acquisition at the center of the dispute.

### Who Is Involved

Tang Tan spent approximately 22 years at Apple in product design roles, working on iPhone and Apple Watch among other hardware lines. He was a vice president of product design, a senior role that gave him direct access to Apple's unannounced product roadmap and proprietary design methodology. He left Apple to found IO Products, an AI hardware startup focused on developing AI-powered consumer devices. OpenAI acquired IO Products in a deal reportedly valued at around $65 billion, one of the largest acquisitions in AI industry history. Following the acquisition, Tan took the role of chief hardware officer at OpenAI, a position created specifically for the IO Products integration. IO Products continues to operate as a separate entity under OpenAI, focused on consumer AI hardware including AI pins, glasses, smart home devices, and health-monitoring wearables.

The lawsuit also names Chang Liu, an engineer who worked at Apple before joining OpenAI. Liu's case forms a distinct thread in the complaint, with Apple alleging he engaged in a pattern of proprietary information access both while employed at Apple and after his departure.

### What Apple Alleges

Apple's complaint alleges that Tang Tan recruited at least twelve senior Apple employees to join IO Products, and that this recruitment was not random. According to the filing, Tan allegedly targeted individuals with access to Apple's most sensitive unannounced products and trade secrets, including details of unreleased hardware and software capabilities. The complaint further alleges that Tan conducted some of these recruitment conversations while using Apple internal codenames for unreleased products, suggesting he drew on his insider knowledge of Apple's roadmap to pitch prospects.

One specific claim in the complaint describes Tan allegedly asking some Apple employees to bring physical Apple products, including batteries and logic boards, to their recruitment interviews. Apple characterizes this as a deliberate effort to extract proprietary hardware knowledge from current employees by having them explain the components in detail. The complaint frames this as part of a broader pattern of using the recruitment process itself as an information-extraction mechanism.

The complaint also alleges that IO Products distributed a document to new hires who came from Apple. This document reportedly instructed recipients on two things: first, how to delay the start of their non-compete obligations by adjusting their departure timeline, and second, how to remove evidence of their OpenAI employment from their social media profiles and public records. Apple claims this was an intentional effort to prevent Apple from discovering the full scope of employee movement and from pursuing legal remedies before the statute of limitations expired.

Separately, the complaint describes Chang Liu's alleged conduct after deciding to leave Apple for OpenAI. Apple alleges Liu did not complete all required exit procedures. He reportedly failed to return his Apple laptop and remained in Apple internal systems after his departure date. The complaint claims Liu exploited a temporary gap in Apple's authentication system to maintain unauthorized access to Apple's internal network for several weeks after his last official work day. During this period, Apple alleges Liu accessed and downloaded proprietary engineering documents, unreleased product specifications, and technical design files.

Apple says it sent a formal warning letter to OpenAI, including documentation of specific allegations, and that OpenAI did not take remedial action to address the concerns or stop the alleged behavior. Apple is seeking damages and an injunction to stop what it characterizes as ongoing trade secret misappropriation. The complaint was filed in the US District Court for the Northern District of California.

### What OpenAI Has Said

OpenAI has publicly denied the allegations and characterized the lawsuit as an attempt to restrict employee mobility and interfere with OpenAI's hardware ambitions. The company has indicated it will defend the case in court. As of July 2026, the case is in its early stages. Apple filed the complaint; OpenAI has not yet filed a formal response, and no court rulings have been made on the merits. The allegations remain allegations, not proven facts.

Trade secret cases often take months or years to resolve, and many settle before reaching trial. Settlement terms can include financial payments, licensing agreements, hiring restrictions, or simply a quiet agreement with no admission of wrongdoing. The fact that Apple pursued litigation rather than a private settlement suggests the company believes it has strong enough evidence to go public.

### Why This Matters Beyond the Two Companies

This lawsuit sits at the intersection of several trends reshaping the AI industry.

First, the AI hardware race is intensifying. Apple has been working on integrating AI more deeply into iPhone and wearables, processing more intelligence on-device rather than sending data to the cloud. OpenAI, through IO Products, is building its own hardware to reduce dependence on phones and app stores, a strategic move that would let the company control the full stack from model to device. When two companies compete to build the same category of product, employee movement between them creates friction. Apple's lawsuit signals it is willing to use litigation to protect what it considers its proprietary knowledge in this race.

Second, the AI industry has hired heavily from established tech companies. Reports suggest more than 400 former Apple employees have joined OpenAI in recent years. That level of employee movement is not unusual in technology. Apple itself has hired heavily from other companies over the decades. But the scale of movement into a direct hardware competitor, combined with the size of the IO Products acquisition, creates legal risk that standard recruitment does not.

Third, the specific allegations, if proven, would represent a meaningful escalation beyond standard trade secret disputes. Asking employees to bring physical proprietary components to interviews, maintaining unauthorized system access after departure, distributing documents that coach new hires on concealing their employment, these go beyond standard employee mobility and into what courts treat as deliberate misappropriation. Courts take these claims seriously when the evidence is specific, which Apple appears to believe it has.

### What to Watch

The case will develop over the coming months. Several things to track. First, whether the court grants Apple a preliminary injunction to stop certain OpenAI hiring or product development activities, which would signal the court takes the allegations seriously. Second, whether the case settles, as many trade secret cases do, with a private agreement and no admission of wrongdoing. Third, whether the lawsuit spills over into regulatory scrutiny of AI industry hiring practices more broadly. Fourth, whether the charges against Chang Liu result in separate criminal referral, since unauthorized computer access can trigger criminal statutes separate from civil trade secret claims. Fifth, how this affects OpenAI's ability to recruit hardware talent going forward, if other companies start demanding stronger contractual protections.

The talent wars in AI are just getting started, and Apple's lawsuit is the most high-profile shot across the bow so far. How it plays out will influence how aggressively companies use litigation to protect proprietary knowledge as the AI industry grows. Whether you see this as a legitimate defense of trade secret rights or an attempt to slow down a competitor may depend on where you stand on employee mobility. But the legal consequences will be real regardless of how the case is viewed.
